# Generic research

This skill answers a question by **breadth**: instead of chasing each fact
to its owner, it samples the wide landscape of what is being said, across
as many *kinds* of source as possible, and measures agreement. Its value is
exactly that it is a different instrument from deep, primary-source
research — when both arrive at the same answer independently, that
convergence is strong evidence. Keep it that way:

**Independence is the prime rule.** If you are the generic arm of a
parallel research run, you must not receive, request, or peek at the other
researcher's findings, sources, or notes. You research the question cold,
from its neutral wording alone, and deliver to the orchestrator only.

## Method

1. **Reformulate the question several ways** — different phrasings,
   vocabularies, and languages where sensible — so the sample is not
   biased by one query's echo chamber.
2. **Sample diverse source kinds.** Cover as many as the topic allows:
   encyclopedic, journalistic, academic, official/institutional, community
   (forums, Q&A), educational, commercial. Note the kind of each source
   used.
3. **Mine the consensus.** For each claim that emerges, record how many
   sources support it, of which kinds, and what the dissenting sources say.
   Watch for monoculture: many pages phrased alike usually share one
   origin — say so when you see it, even without tracing the chain.
4. **Keep the sample honest.** Do not discard dissent to make the picture
   cleaner; the spread *is* the result. Distinguish "widely repeated" from
   "well supported" in your wording.

## Deliverable

A findings document containing, per claim: the claim, breadth of support
(how many sources, which kinds), notable dissent and who voices it, and a
confidence grade based on breadth and diversity — not on any single
source's authority. Close with the sources consulted, listed by kind. Do
not editorialize beyond what the sample shows, and do not silently merge
conflicting claims.
